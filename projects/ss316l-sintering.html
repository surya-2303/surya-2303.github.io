Background

The Question We Started With

Metal 3D printing is usually gated behind expensive powder-bed systems — SLM, EBM — that require inert atmospheres, high-power lasers, and significant capital infrastructure. This project asked whether you could use a standard filament-based FDM printer (the kind normally used for plastics) to produce real metallic components, provided you follow the print with solvent debinding and sintering.

SS316L was the material of choice: its corrosion resistance, biocompatibility, and wide industrial use make it a meaningful target. The filament is a composite of stainless steel powder suspended in a polymer binder — the FDM process forms the geometry, and the two post-processing stages burn away the polymer and consolidate the metal particles into a dense, fully metallic part.

Objective: investigate how layer thickness, infill density, and print speed influence porosity and final part quality in FDM-printed SS316L — and characterize the result after debinding and sintering.
Stage 1

3D Printing the Green Part

The printer was a CreatBot F430, a high-temperature FDM machine with a hardened nozzle capable of 420 °C — necessary for metal-loaded filament. SS316L filament was sourced from NextGeneration 3D Printers, Chennai.

Early prints at higher layer thicknesses and speeds came out badly: warping, layer separation, and poor adhesion. Iterating through parameter combinations led to the following optimized settings:

ParameterOptimized ValueNotes
Layer Thickness0.15 mmInitial 1 mm caused severe warping
Infill Density100%+ 120% flow rate on first 2 layers
Print Speed30 mm/s50 mm/s produced delamination

A non-obvious fix: the slicer at 100% infill would leave small voids at sharp nozzle-turn corners. Boosting flow rate to 120% for the first two layers closed those gaps without over-extruding the rest of the part.

Specimens were designed in SolidWorks to ASTM E8 (tensile) and ASTM D790 (flexural) standards, plus two real-world geometry tests — an IKEA hook and a shelf bracket — to observe how complex shapes behaved through the full process.

Stages 2 & 3

Debinding and Sintering at TANSAM

The CMRIT lab lacked a controlled-atmosphere furnace — non-negotiable for sintering metal parts, since oxidation at high temperature ruins the part. The team sourced the facility from TANSAM (Tamil Nadu Smart and Advanced Manufacturing Centre), which runs a Markforged Metal X system.

1

Solvent Debinding — Markforged Wash-1

Green parts were submerged in Opteon SF-79 solvent for 16 hours at 120 °C. The solvent dissolves the primary polymer binder while leaving the metal powder network intact. Parts then dried for 4 hours. At this stage they are called brown parts — fragile and porous, but holding geometry.

2

Sintering — Markforged Sinter-2

Brown parts were loaded into the tube furnace under a 97% Argon / 3% Hydrogen controlled atmosphere. Temperature was ramped to 1360 °C — within the optimal 1290–1370 °C window for SS316L — and held for 16 hours. Metal powder particles bond through atomic diffusion, eliminating porosity and producing a fully metallic component.

Results

Shrinkage Analysis

Sintering consolidates porosity — which means the part physically shrinks. Comparing CAD nominal dimensions against post-sinter measured averages revealed anisotropic shrinkage across all three axes:

~17%
Length axis
Y
~13%
Width axis
Z
~21%
Thickness axis

The Z-axis shrinkage being highest is consistent with layer-stacking: inter-layer binder removal leaves more void volume in the build direction than in-plane. These figures give a direct scaling factor for future designs — a part intended to be 100 mm long should be modelled at ~117 mm before printing.

Characterization

Defects and Microstructure

Post-sinter optical microscopy (AX10, 10× and 50×) revealed two defect types. SEM imaging at BMS College, Bengaluru confirmed that overall porosity had reduced significantly after sintering, but not fully eliminated.

Observed

Layer Delamination

Visible in flexural specimens at 10×. Layers had not fully bonded, creating planar separations. Likely cause: insufficient inter-layer adhesion during printing, amplified by anisotropic shrinkage stress during sintering.

Observed

Surface Bubble Formation

Found in tensile specimens at 50×. Indicative of residual binder that didn't fully evacuate during debinding — trapped volatiles expanding during the sintering thermal ramp. Points to binder composition or debind cycle duration as the improvement lever.

Both defects were minor and didn't compromise the overall result — the required post-processing was achieved. They do, however, mark two clear paths for improvement in follow-on work.

Simulation

FEA — Structural Behaviour

ANSYS Workbench — Static Structural

Tensile and compression tests were simulated using SS316L material properties. Von Mises stress and strain distributions were computed across the gauge section of the ASTM E8 specimens. Stress–strain curves from both load cases show elastic behaviour up to approximately 275 MPa, consistent with literature values for sintered SS316L — which typically falls below wrought or SLM equivalents due to residual porosity.

The simulation established a theoretical performance baseline to compare against physical test data once destructive testing is conducted — a standard validation workflow for AM part qualification.

Reflections

Key Takeaways

🔬

FDM + sintering is viable for metal parts

The process produced genuine stainless steel components at a fraction of powder-bed fusion costs. The trade-off is tighter process control and slightly lower final density.

📐

Shrinkage must be designed in, not calibrated out

Anisotropic shrinkage of 13–21% is predictable and consistent. Future parts should apply axis-specific scale factors at the CAD stage before printing.

⚗️

Binder quality is the hidden variable

Both observed defects trace back to the binder system, not the metal powder or sintering conditions. Filament sourcing and shelf-life are the practical levers to pull.

🏭

Infrastructure constraints are an engineering problem too

No controlled-atmosphere furnace on campus meant outsourcing to TANSAM. Scoping around available infrastructure and building external partnerships is real-world engineering.
